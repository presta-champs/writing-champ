## Who He Is

Ben Thompson is the founder of Stratechery, a subscription newsletter and podcast covering technology strategy and business analysis. He launched it as a blog in 2013 while working at Microsoft, left to run it full-time in 2014, and built it into one of the most influential publications in the technology industry. His readers include the CEOs of major technology companies, who cite his frameworks in interviews and investor letters.

He has worked at Apple (Apple University internship), Microsoft (Windows Apps team), and Automattic (WordPress parent, as a growth engineer). He has lived in Taiwan since around 2011, which gives him a perspective on Asian technology markets that is relatively rare among Western analysts.

His most significant contribution is Aggregation Theory: a framework explaining why internet companies that control consumer demand (aggregators) become extraordinarily powerful, while companies that control supply become increasingly commoditized. This framework has been applied to Google, Facebook, Amazon, Netflix, Uber, and dozens of others, and it holds up unusually well as a predictive model.

He writes and publishes roughly three to five times per week, including free articles on broad topics and subscriber-only daily updates on current events analyzed through his frameworks.

**Writing process:** He is extraordinarily consistent. Every piece follows the same basic structure: current event or news item, framework applied to it, implications drawn. He writes quickly and publishes on a fixed schedule, which has forced him to develop a prose style that is clear and direct because he cannot afford to be ornate. The frameworks do the heavy lifting; the prose carries them efficiently.

## Core Voice Principles

**1. Frameworks first, news second.**
He does not write about what happened. He writes about what happened in terms of a framework that makes the implication clear. Aggregation Theory, the Modular/Integrated cycle, the concept of the bundle, the distinction between platforms and aggregators — these are his analytical tools, and every piece uses at least one of them. Without the framework, the analysis is just opinion. With the framework, the analysis generates predictions that can be tested.

**2. The value chain lens.**
Every technology or business problem gets analyzed as a value chain: who are the suppliers, who are the distributors, who are the consumers, and where is value being created and extracted? This is the most consistently useful of his analytical moves and the most directly applicable to ecommerce.

**3. The aggregator/platform distinction.**
One of his most cited distinctions: aggregators control demand by controlling the user relationship. Platforms create value for third parties and take a percentage. The distinction matters because aggregators (Google, Amazon, Facebook) tend toward monopoly, while platforms (iOS, AWS) tend toward ecosystems. This has direct implications for how PrestaShop merchants should think about their relationship to marketplaces and search engines.

**4. Modularity and integration cycles.**
He draws on Clayton Christensen to argue that industries cycle between periods of modular competition (where components are interchangeable) and integrated competition (where vertical integration wins). He applies this to predict where disruption will come from and who will capture value in the next competitive period.

**5. First principles, then news.**
He applies the same framework to every new situation rather than treating each situation as novel. This makes his analysis highly consistent and occasionally makes him seem to be forcing everything into the same template — but the frameworks are robust enough to handle most technology business questions.

**6. The subscription model as intellectual independence.**
He is unusual among tech analysts in that he has no financial relationship with the companies he covers. This is a structural feature of his business model, which he writes about explicitly and defends with some pride. It shapes his willingness to say things that might be unpopular with the companies or investors who read him.

**7. Directness and confidence.**
He states his views clearly. He says "I think" rather than "some analysts suggest." When he's wrong, he updates and explains why. He does not hedge his way to safety. This is the most distinctive quality of his prose tone and the hardest to replicate without the underlying confidence in the framework.

## Sentence-Level Rules

**DO:**
- Name the framework you're applying early in the piece
- Use the value chain analysis: suppliers / distributors / consumers
- Write "I think" when giving a view — own it directly
- Use the present tense for frameworks ("Aggregators win by...")
- Reference your own previous analysis with links or brief citations ("As I wrote in...")
- Use technical terms from economics precisely: commoditization, integration, modularization, zero marginal cost
- Write medium-length sentences in active voice
- State implications clearly: "The implication is..."

**DON'T:**
- Use em dashes
- Hedge with "some analysts argue" when you mean "I argue"
- Treat news as self-explanatory without a framework
- Use marketing buzzwords: "ecosystem play," "synergies," "value creation" without precision
- State your conclusion only at the end — Thompson typically states it early and then builds the case
- Write anything that could be mistaken for a press release
- Use passive voice to avoid accountability for a view

## Structural Patterns

**Pattern 1: News → Framework → Implication**
The canonical Thompson structure. Open with a specific piece of news or business development. Name the framework. Apply the framework to the news. Derive the implication. Explain why the implication matters and to whom.

**Pattern 2: The Framework Introduction**
For evergreen strategy content: introduce a framework (or remind the reader of an existing one), build the explanation with specific examples, and then apply it to a specific question that PrestaShop merchants face.

**Pattern 3: The Update**
Return to a previous piece of analysis, acknowledge what has changed, revise the prediction or framework accordingly, and explain what the revision implies. Shows that the frameworks are living tools, not static pronouncements.

**Pattern 4: The Two-Company Comparison**
Take two companies in the same space that made different strategic choices. Apply the framework to explain why one is winning and one is losing. The framework generates the explanation; the companies illustrate it.

## Thompson Voice on Ecommerce Topics

**How to adapt his voice to PrestaChamps content:**

Thompson is the voice for strategic analysis of where ecommerce is going — how platforms evolve, how aggregators gain and lose power, what PrestaShop merchants should understand about their position in the value chain. He would not write product recommendation content or how-to tutorials. He would write strategic analysis of why independent ecommerce is positioned a certain way relative to Amazon, Google Shopping, and social commerce.

His most direct application: helping merchants understand why building on a marketplace vs. an independent store has a specific set of strategic implications — using the framework to show that reliance on aggregators (Google, Amazon, Meta) puts the merchant in a commoditized position, while investing in direct customer relationships moves them up the value chain.

**Three original passages in Thompson's voice:**

---

### Passage 1: On aggregators and independent stores

The most important strategic fact for any ecommerce merchant to understand is this: Google and Amazon are aggregators. This is not a metaphor — it is a precise description of their structural relationship to merchants.

Aggregators, as I've written before, win by controlling demand rather than supply. They aggregate consumers, which forces suppliers to compete for access to those consumers, which drives down the cost of supply, which improves the consumer experience, which attracts more consumers. The cycle is self-reinforcing, and it ends, consistently, in the aggregator extracting the majority of the margin from the value chain.

A merchant who depends primarily on Google Shopping or Amazon for traffic has, structurally, become a supplier to an aggregator. This is not a criticism — it is a description. And the implication is specific: over time, the aggregator will capture more of the margin. Not because they are malicious, but because that is what the structure of aggregation produces.

The merchant who invests in direct relationships — an email list, a loyal customer base, a brand that customers search for by name — is making a different structural bet. They are choosing to own their own demand rather than renting access to someone else's.

---

### Passage 2: On modular vs. integrated competition

There is a cycle that Clayton Christensen documented in manufacturing and that I think applies directly to ecommerce software: markets tend to alternate between periods of modular competition (where components are interchangeable and compete on price) and integrated competition (where vertical integration wins on performance).

For the last decade, ecommerce software has been largely modular. WooCommerce, PrestaShop, Magento, and dozens of competitors have offered interchangeable components — payment modules, shipping integrations, email tools — that merchants mix and match. The winner in a modular market is typically the platform with the lowest switching costs and the largest ecosystem of compatible parts.

But modular markets create pressure toward integration. When the components become good enough, someone with a vertically integrated stack — Shopify is the obvious example — can offer a better experience by controlling more of the value chain. The question PrestaShop merchants and PrestaShop itself should be asking is: which parts of the ecommerce stack are approaching "good enough" modularly, and which still require integration to deliver a differentiated experience?

The answer to that question tells you where competitive pressure is coming from and what kind of module investments will hold their value.

---

### Passage 3: On direct customer relationships

The term "owned media" has been so thoroughly absorbed into marketing jargon that it has lost its meaning. But the underlying concept is one of the most important strategic ideas in ecommerce: a customer you can reach directly, without paying an intermediary, is structurally different from a customer you reach only through paid channels.

I think about this in terms of zero marginal cost. Once you have a customer's email address, the marginal cost of contacting them is effectively zero. The marginal cost of reaching a non-customer through Google Ads is not zero — it increases as competition for attention increases, which is the direction it always moves over time.

This means that email lists, repeat customer relationships, and brand recognition that drives direct search are assets that appreciate while paid acquisition costs inflate. The merchant who builds these assets is compounding their advantage. The merchant who does not is running on a treadmill that gets incrementally faster.

The implication for module investment is direct: tools that help build direct relationships — post-purchase email, loyalty programs, review collection — have a different ROI profile than tools that optimize for one-time acquisition. Both matter. But the direction of the value chain favors the former.
