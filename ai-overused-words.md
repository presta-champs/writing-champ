# AI-Overused Words and Phrases
### A comprehensive reference for the persona blocklist

Compiled from GPTZero research, Embryo's cross-model analysis, Pangram Labs detection studies, academic research on LLM vocabulary patterns, and direct observation across ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, DeepSeek, and others. Last updated March 2025.

Use this list as the foundation for every persona's **Forbidden Words** field. The more of these a piece avoids, the more human it reads.

---

## Why these words exist

LLMs are trained on massive datasets of internet text, academic papers, and corporate writing. Through reinforcement learning, they "overfit" to patterns that scored well during training — formal, hedging, authoritative-sounding language that reads as competent but not human. The result is a recognizable AI accent: words that no real person uses in casual conversation, phrases that pad rather than add meaning, and structural habits that betray machine logic.

Some of these words appear **100x more often** in AI writing than in human writing (source: GPTZero). Removing them alone won't make content undetectable, but it dramatically reduces editing time and makes the writing feel lived-in.

## Section 11: Structural and Formatting Habits
*Not individual words — but patterns that signal AI as clearly as any phrase.*

- Opening every article with a broad statement about how important the topic is before saying anything specific
- Ending every article with a summary of what was just said
- Starting bullet-point lists with a sentence like "Here are [N] key [things]:"
- Three-part structures applied to everything regardless of whether they fit
- Adding "In conclusion" before a conclusion paragraph
- Using bold text on random phrases mid-paragraph as if highlighting a textbook
- Numbering points when no order or ranking is implied
- Inserting a FAQ section that answers questions no one asked
- Every section beginning with a topic sentence that restates the section heading
- Adding a "Final thoughts" or "Key takeaways" section at the end of short articles
- Starting responses with the phrase "Great question!" or "Absolutely!"
- Paragraphs that end by "setting up" the next paragraph ("Now that we've covered X, let's explore Y...")
