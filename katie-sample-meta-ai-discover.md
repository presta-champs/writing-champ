# Writing Sample: Katie Notopoulos
## "Mark Zuckerberg has created the saddest place on the internet with Meta AI's public feed"
### Business Insider, June 2025

---

## Sample Classification

**Type:** Platform investigation with immersive consumer testing
**Structure:** First-person observation log with accountability conclusion
**Voice register:** Drier than her product testing pieces — the comedy is darker here because the subject is darker
**Key technique demonstrated:** Escalating emotional stakes delivered through flat, sequential reporting

---

## Annotated Full Text

> *Meta's stand-alone AI app launched in late April, and like many people, my first thought was, "Huh?" After trying it out a little, my next thought was, "Oh no." A few weeks later, after the initial surge of curious new users, my thought is now: "Dear God."*

**What this demonstrates:** The three-beat escalation used as a lede. Each beat is one word or two, delivered flat. The comedy is structural — the progression from confusion to dread is the joke, and it lands because she doesn't explain it. She trusts the reader to feel the escalation. No adjectives. No "I was disturbed to find." Just the sequence.

---

> *Meta AI's public stream is perhaps the most depressing feed I've come across in a long time.*

**What this demonstrates:** She will make a direct evaluative claim when the evidence supports it. "Perhaps the most depressing" is not hedged into uselessness — it is her actual conclusion. She earns it in the paragraphs that follow. The claim comes first. The receipts follow.

---

> *I'm not sure why someone would want to share some of the things I've read — nor am I sure why anyone who's not a nosy weirdo like I am would want to look at a feed of a bunch of strangers' interactions with a chatbot. And yet, people do share.*

**What this demonstrates:** Self-implication as a voice tool. She calls herself a nosy weirdo before anyone else can. This is not false modesty — it is honest. She was, in fact, spending significant time reading strangers' AI conversations. Naming it disarms the critique and also, quietly, is funny. The sentence "And yet, people do share" is a pivot done in five words.

---

> *Back in late April, after the initial launch, Meta AI's feed was indeed mostly images. But mixed in, I noticed a few conversations that seemed more personal — someone asking about vitamin supplements for a 65-year-old woman, or for legal advice about getting fired, or for a special prayer.*

**What this demonstrates:** The list as evidence. Three items, in ascending emotional weight. "Vitamin supplements" is benign. "Legal advice about getting fired" is more personal. "A special prayer" lands last because it is the most intimate and the most unexpected. She did not label this list as "increasingly personal." She ordered it so the reader feels that.

---

> *When I checked back on the app more recently, I was seeing even more of these personal takes — some even including a person's phone number and email address when they asked for help drafting a letter to a judge in a child custody case.*

**What this demonstrates:** The specific detail as the argument. She does not say "some posts included sensitive personal information." She says phone number, email address, letter to a judge, child custody case. The specificity does more work than any editorial comment she could add.

---

> *Did that person mean to share that information with the world? Maybe, but I'm guessing they might have clicked the wrong button and didn't realize what they were doing. I could be wrong!*

**What this demonstrates:** The honest hedge — distinct from diplomatic hedging. She genuinely does not know whether this person shared accidentally. She says she guesses. She acknowledges she could be wrong. This is not her softening a critique — it is accurate reporting of her actual epistemic state. The exclamation mark on "I could be wrong!" is the driest possible delivery of that concession.

---

> *Other conversations I saw: someone asking the AI to send them a reminder at the end of the month to cancel their Experian credit-monitoring subscription.*

**What this demonstrates:** The observational aside that lands through pure specificity. This line is funny because of "Experian credit-monitoring subscription" — the brand name, the specific financial anxiety it implies, the fact that this person turned to a public AI chatbot to manage a recurring billing problem. She does not explain why this is funny. She describes it exactly.

---

> *At least two recordings I listened to appeared to be from people who didn't realize they had hit the audio button. One was having a conversation with a coworker about their shift schedule. Another sounded like a pocket dial: A man was having a conversation with another person, and Meta AI kept chiming in with suggestions.*

**What this demonstrates:** "At least two" — she counted. Specificity as credibility. The pocket dial detail is perfect Notopoulos: the image is absurd (a man mid-conversation while an AI helpfully offers suggestions) but she describes it completely straight. No "absurdly," no "somehow." Just what happened.

---

> *I reached out to more than 20 people whose posts I'd come across in the feed to ask them about their experience; I heard back from one, who told me that he hadn't intended to make his chat with the bot public. (He was asking for car repair advice.)*

**What this demonstrates:** The parenthetical at its best. The car repair detail is not necessary for the argument — the point is made by "he hadn't intended to make his chat with the bot public." But "(He was asking for car repair advice.)" is the flat final note that makes the human dimension of the story land. It is very normal. It is a normal person asking about their car. It is on a public feed they didn't know was public. That gap is the whole story in four words.

---

> *And yet, the consumer-facing features using Meta AI are kind of, uh, meh.*

**What this demonstrates:** She will use colloquial interjections ("uh," "meh") sparingly and precisely. "Meh" is the right word here — not "underwhelming" or "disappointing." Meh. It dismisses without performing contempt. The "uh" is a spoken-language pause rendered in text, which slows the sentence down just enough to make "meh" land harder.

---

> *If Meta wants its users to adopt its AI like it's a useful and great thing, it's hard to square this with my awful experience on the Meta AI app.*

**What this demonstrates:** The closing verdict as a direct challenge to the company. "My awful experience" is stated plainly. She does not reach for a more journalistic formulation. It was awful. That is her conclusion. The word "square" is doing quiet structural work — it implies incoherence in Meta's position without her having to argue the case at length. The case has already been made. This sentence is the summary.

---

## Structural Notes for Replication

**The lede:** Three reactions, escalating, no adjectives. The escalation is the structure.

**The evidence pattern:** Observation → specific instance → specific instance → specific instance → minimal editorial comment. The instances carry the argument.

**The accountability move:** She contacted more than 20 people. Got one response. Reports both numbers honestly. Does not pretend the response rate was better than it was.

**The Meta relationship:** She quotes Meta's spokesman by name and represents his position accurately ("there's a multi-step process to sharing"). She does not misrepresent the company's defense. Then she continues reporting. The defense does not change what she observed.

**What she does not do:** She does not conclude with a call to action ("Meta should..."). She does not prescribe a fix. She reports what she found, names why it's a problem, and ends with her experience stated plainly. The reader can draw their own conclusions about what Meta should do.

---

## Sentences to Study

These are the sentences from this piece that most precisely demonstrate her techniques. Study the construction, not the content.

1. *"my thought is now: 'Dear God.'"* — escalation landing at maximum compression
2. *"I'm guessing they might have clicked the wrong button and didn't realize what they were doing. I could be wrong!"* — honest epistemic hedging, not diplomatic hedging
3. *"(He was asking for car repair advice.)"* — the parenthetical that reframes everything above it
4. *"And yet, the consumer-facing features using Meta AI are kind of, uh, meh."* — colloquial precision, the "uh" as timing device
5. *"it's a jumble of unrelated, random stuff, most of which actually is pretty boring."* — the honest admission that undercuts the dramatic framing without destroying it

## Application to Ecommerce Topics

This piece's structure — enter the space, observe, collect specific instances, reach out to participants, report findings, render verdict — maps directly onto:

- Testing a new marketplace feature or seller tool (enter it, use it, find the edge cases, report them)
- Investigating what merchants are actually saying in a Facebook group or Reddit community (read the feed, collect the specific posts, note what they reveal)
- Reviewing a platform policy change from the merchant's perspective (read the policy, test it in practice, find where the reality diverges from the announcement)
- Examining what AI tools for merchants actually do with merchant data (privacy and UX investigation in the Notopoulos mode)

The voice does not require a stunt. It requires genuine engagement with the subject and honest reporting of what you find.
